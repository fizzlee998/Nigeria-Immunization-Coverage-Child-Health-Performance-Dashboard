Policy Implications

Why Low Immunization Threatens Child Survival

Nigeria accounts for approximately 2.2 million zero-dose children — the second highest globally (WHO/UNICEF WUENIC 2022). This analysis confirms a statistically significant negative correlation (r ≈ -0.78, p < 0.001) between DPT3 coverage and under-5 mortality. States with the lowest immunization consistently record the highest rates of child death from preventable diseases including measles, pertussis, and tetanus.

The North West and North East geopolitical zones — containing all 10 lowest-performing states — account for the majority of Nigeria's vaccine-preventable child deaths. In Zamfara, Kebbi, and Sokoto, where DPT3 coverage is below 20%, under-5 mortality rates exceed 240 per 1,000 live births, more than four times the rate in high-performing southern states.

Priority States for Immediate Intervention

Zamfara, Kebbi, Yobe, Sokoto, Katsina, Jigawa, Borno, Bauchi, Taraba, and Kano. These states show persistent underperformance across all survey rounds from 2015–2022 and carry the highest burden of zero-dose children in Nigeria.

How Data Improves Resource Allocation

DHIS2 administrative data combined with DHS survey estimates enables LGA-level targeting of mobile vaccination teams. Real-time dashboards like this one allow programme directors to identify micro-pockets of underperformance before they become epidemiological crises. Investment in data quality — particularly addressing administrative over-reporting — will sharpen targeting of the NPHCDA Reach Every Ward strategy.

Policy Recommendations

Practical Recommendations for Federal and State Agencies

  • Emergency mobile vaccination teams deployed immediately to the 10 lowest-performing states, prioritising Zamfara, Kebbi, and Sokoto where coverage is below 20%.
  • Cold chain infrastructure investment — solar-powered cold storage at PHCs across the North West and North East where power supply is most unreliable.
  • Community mobilisation — engage Islamic scholars, Emirs, and traditional birth attendants to address vaccine hesitancy at the community level.
  • Conditional federal health transfers — link health allocations to independently verified LGA-level immunization coverage targets.
  • DHIS2 data quality programme — implement real-time validation and community-based verification to eliminate administrative over-reporting.
  • Postpartum immunization touchpoints — use antenatal and postnatal contacts to initiate and complete infant immunization schedules.
  • Cross-sector strategies — address female education, poverty, and physical access as structural drivers of immunization inequity.
